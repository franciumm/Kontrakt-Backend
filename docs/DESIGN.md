## Problem Statement

Freelancers get burned by bad contracts constantly. The painful patterns repeat: scope creep from "unlimited revisions," work-for-hire traps that strip portfolio rights, missing kill fees that mean zero pay when a client ghosts at v3, late-payment terms with no teeth, IP assignment clauses written in the client's favor, jurisdiction mismatches that make disputes uneconomic to pursue.

The user's framing: an AI site where a freelancer describes their gig, gets asked sharp questions about the details that matter, and ends up with a contract that actually covers them. The detail-coverage angle is the differentiated part — most existing "AI contract generators" template-fill; almost none interrogate.

This is a hackathon build. The goal is a 60-second "whoa" demo, not a shipped product. The combined-swing version: interrogator spine + exposure dial + real-clause library + live reshape animation.

## What Makes This Cool

The "whoa" moment: a freelancer types one sentence about their gig, watches a red exposure dial climb down toward green as each sharp follow-up question closes a real legal gap, and sees a contract materialize live with each answer. By the end of 60 seconds they have a contract AND a one-page exposure report that names every clause, what it does in plain English, and what would have happened without it.

Three layers of "whoa" stacked:
1. **Conversational interrogation** — the AI asks questions a lawyer would ask, not a form would. "You didn't mention a kill fee. What happens if they cancel at round 2?"
2. **Visual risk score** — the exposure dial moving red → green IS the demo. Universally legible in 5 seconds.
3. **Live graph visualization** — the clause dependency graph updates in real time as the freelancer answers. Bonus demo moment, reinforces "this is genuinely how lawyers think about contracts."

## Constraints

- **48-hour hackathon** (team of 2). Real build budget after sleep/food/demo-prep: ~30 hours of focused work.
- **Stack locked:** Next.js (App Router) + Vercel + Tailwind + GLM-5.2 API (OpenAI SDK). One-command deploy to a real URL judges can poke.
- **Solo-role separation needed:** with 2 people and zero margin, both must ship in parallel with sharp ownership boundaries. No collision on the same files.
- **Demo conditions rule everything:** latency must stay under 2 seconds per round, no flaky LLM outputs under live conditions, every demo path must be reproducible.
- **No legal credentials:** the team can't credibly claim "lawyer-drafted." Must lean on public-domain templates for clause credibility.

## Premises

The user agreed to all four load-bearing premises:

1. **The exposure dial must be honest, not theatrical.** It reflects actual clause coverage — each critical gap (kill fee, IP, payment, late fees, jurisdiction, dispute, revisions) contributes to exposure. A real risk model under the hood, not a progress bar in costume.
2. **The clause library starts from public-domain templates, not from scratch.** Writing 20–30 lawyer-grade clauses in 48 hours is impossible. Creative Commons / open-source legal sources (Stanford CodeX, Common Form, state bar associations) provide a credentialed baseline. AI adapts per gig type + jurisdiction. Credibility comes from the source, not from the team drafting.
3. **The output is a contract PLUS a one-page exposure report.** The report (what's covered, what's still gaps, plain-English per-clause explanations) is what makes the demo feel like a real product. A contract alone is just a document generator.
4. **Judges need a deployed URL, not a screen-share.** Vercel deploy. Worth the 1–2 hours of setup. Hackathon demos die when judges can't touch.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Template-driven interrogator (Minimal viable)

Clauses as JSON/TS data objects with metadata (gig-type triggers, exposure weights, dependencies). Questions hardcoded as a branching wizard. LLM assembles + adapts the final contract from selected clauses + user answers.

- Effort: M (15–20 hrs) · Risk: Low
- Pros: most predictable demo path; fastest to ship; honest exposure dial (you authored the weights)
- Cons: feels less "AI-magical"; can't handle gig types you didn't script

### Approach B: Clause-graph walker (Ideal architecture) — CHOSEN

Clauses as nodes in a dependency graph (e.g., "kill-fee clause depends on payment-terms being set"). Questionnaire branches adaptively based on which clauses the gig type triggers. LLM walks the graph, generates context-aware transitions, produces a contract that flows rather than concatenated clauses.

- Effort: L (25–30 hrs) · Risk: Med
- Pros: most elegant (matches how lawyers actually think); dynamic (handles unanticipated gig types via graph routing); bonus demo moment from live-rendered graph visualization
- Cons: more upfront design work (graph schema, clause metadata, dependency rules); harder to debug under time pressure

### Approach C: Two-agent interro-auditor (Creative / lateral)

Two LLM calls per round — one ("interrogator") asks the next sharp question; another ("auditor") scans the current draft for gaps the interrogator missed and feeds them back. Exposure dial driven by the auditor's gap-detection, not a static checklist.

- Effort: L (28–35 hrs) · Risk: High
- Pros: maximum "AI magic" (two agents arguing live is the most memorable demo); most adaptive
- Cons: latency stacks (two calls per round = slower UX); consistency under demo conditions is genuinely hard to control; high risk of hallucinated gaps making the team look bad live

## Recommended Approach

**Approach B: Clause-graph walker.** Delivers the "go all out" ambition with controlled demo risk. The graph is the right mental model — contracts really are dependency graphs of clauses — and the live graph visualization is a free second "whoa" moment on top of the exposure dial. A is too thin for the team's stated ambition; C is too risky under live demo conditions.

## Resolved Decisions (pre-committed)

These were open questions in the first draft. The adversarial spec review caught that leaving them open would block Person A's critical path. Pre-committing now.

1. **Clause library source: Stanford CodeX (Common Form DB).** Primary source. Permissive licensing, machine-readable clause text, designed exactly for this kind of assembly. **Licensing check at Hour 0–2:** Person A audits each pulled clause's license tag. If any clause is blocked or ambiguous, Person A drafts a replacement clause at authoring time and labels it `illustrative — not legal advice` in the UI. **Person A starts here at hour 0, not hour 2.**
2. **Gig-type coverage: 2 for the demo, not 3.** Logo/design + software development. Two gig types with 6–8 polished clause nodes each beats three half-baked ones. The graph still demonstrates routing across different gig shapes (design has portfolio-usage clauses; software has acceptance-testing clauses).
3. **Clause schema (locked):**
   ```ts
   type ClauseNode = {
     id: string;                    // 'kill-fee'
     gigTypes: GigType[];           // ['design', 'software']
     title: string;                 // 'Kill Fee'
     body: string;                  // the actual clause text, adapted from CC source
     plainEnglish: string;          // 'If client cancels after v2, they pay 50% of the fee.'
     exposureWeight: number;        // 0..10 (integer scale; sum of answered weights / total applicable weights = coverage %)
     triggersWhen: (state) => bool; // when this clause becomes relevant
     dependsOn: string[];           // ['payment-terms'] — must be answered first
     questions: Question[];         // AUTHORED ON-NODE (Person A writes these), not LLM-generated
   }
   type Question = { id: string; prompt: string; field: string; inputType: 'text'|'choice'|'number'; }
   ```
   `getNextQuestions(answeredState, gigType)` walks the graph: returns the next 1–3 questions from clauses where `triggersWhen(state) === true` AND all `dependsOn` are satisfied AND the question's `field` is not yet in `answeredState`. **Questions are authored on-node** (deterministic, reproducible demo) — the LLM is only used for the final contract assembly, never for question generation.
4. **Exposure weight scale: 0–10 integer per clause.** Coverage % = (sum of `exposureWeight` for clauses with all questions answered) / (sum of `exposureWeight` for all triggered clauses). The dial maps 0–100% to red→yellow→green. Person A assigns weights during clause authoring based on consequence severity (e.g., 'payment-terms' = 10, 'jurisdiction' = 7, 'dispute-resolution' = 5).
5. **Demo contingency:** We rely on fast, reliable endpoints (`gemma-4-26b-a4b-it` and `glm-5p2`) and robust error handling rather than cache fallbacks. Cache fallbacks have been rejected to ensure realistic demo output.

## Success Criteria

A successful hackathon demo, in order of priority:

1. **The 60-second "whoa" lands.** A judge watches the freelancer type one sentence, sees the exposure dial move red → green, sees the contract materialize, and gets the "this is genuinely useful" feeling. This is the must-hit bar.
2. **Deployed URL works.** Judges can poke it on their phones after the pitch. Vercel URL live and stable.
3. **The graph visualization updates in real time.** Bonus moment — the dependency graph renders live as answers come in.
4. **At least 2 gig types demoable** (logo design + software development) with 6–8 clause nodes per gig type in the graph.
5. **The exposure report renders as a shareable one-pager** alongside the contract — judges can see "this is what was covered, this is what wasn't."

Stretch:
- Plain-English explanation per clause ("this says: if they don't pay in 30 days, you keep the IP")
- Live edit: judge types a different gig description and the wizard adapts. **Conditional:** only if hour 40+ and the scripted demo is stable. Unscripted gig-type routing through the graph walker has not been pressure-tested — don't risk the primary demo on it. **Fallback if a judge enters an unsupported gig type:** default to the design graph, label clearly in the UI as "routing as design — beta."

### Hour 36–48: Buffer + contingency
